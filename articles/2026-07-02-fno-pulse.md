## Decoding today's open interest

**Open Interest (OI)** refers to the total number of outstanding derivative contracts, such as options or futures, that have not yet been settled. For options, the strike price with the highest Call OI often acts as a significant resistance level, as many participants have sold calls at that strike, expecting the price to stay below it. Conversely, the strike price with the highest Put OI typically acts as a strong support level, as many participants have sold puts there, anticipating the price to remain above it.

Today, for the Nifty 50, the heaviest Put OI was observed at 24,000, which is 0.7% below the spot price. This suggests that option writers are positioning for 24,000 to act as a strong floor. Fresh Put writing, which indicates new put sellers entering the market, was concentrated near 24,150, reinforcing the expectation of support at or above these levels.

On the Sensex, the heaviest Call OI was at 77,500, precisely where the index closed. This level is seen as a significant resistance. However, fresh Put writing was notable near 77,400, suggesting that option sellers believe this level will hold as support. Conversely, fresh Call writing near 77,500 indicates that call sellers expect the index to face a cap around this mark.

For Bank Nifty, the heaviest Put OI was at 58,000, which is very close to the spot price, indicating immediate support. Fresh Call writing was observed near 58,533, suggesting that call sellers anticipate resistance around this level. However, fresh Put writing near 57,633 also points to a belief in a floor at lower levels.

## PCR — what the put-call ratio is whispering

The **Put-Call Ratio (PCR)** is a widely used indicator that compares the number of put options traded to the number of call options traded. A PCR above 1 generally suggests a bullish sentiment, as more puts are being bought or written (sold) than calls, indicating that participants are either hedging against downside or expecting prices to rise. A PCR below 1 often suggests a bearish sentiment.

For the Nifty 50, the PCR based on total Open Interest (PCR(OI)) stood at 1.2, indicating a balanced to slightly bullish book. More significantly, today's flow PCR (ΔPCR), which considers only the fresh Open Interest added today, was a robust 5.78. This exceptionally high figure suggests that today's fresh option writing was heavily skewed towards puts, with put sellers expecting the market to find support and move higher.

The Sensex recorded a PCR(OI) of 1.16, also suggesting a relatively balanced to mildly bullish positioning. Its ΔPCR for today's flow was 1.33, indicating that fresh option writers were leaning towards selling more puts than calls, which historically tends to coincide with expectations of stability or upward movement.

Bank Nifty's PCR(OI) was 0.79, suggesting a slightly bearish overall book. However, its ΔPCR for today's flow was 0.54, indicating that today's fresh writing was more call-heavy. This means more call options were sold compared to put options, which historically tends to coincide with expectations of resistance or a cap on upward movement.

## Max pain and the magnet effect

**Max Pain** is the strike price at which option writers (those who sell options) would experience the least financial loss if the underlying asset expires at that price. It's often considered a magnetic point, as the market price has historically tended to gravitate towards this level as expiry approaches, particularly for weekly or monthly contracts.

For the Nifty 50, the Max Pain strike is at 24,000, which is 0.7% below the current spot price. This suggests a potential pull towards this level by expiry.

The Sensex has its Max Pain at 77,400, which is very close to the current spot price. This indicates that option writers are positioned for the index to expire around current levels, acting as a strong magnet.

Bank Nifty's Max Pain is at 58,600, which is 1.0% above the current spot price. This suggests a potential upward pull towards this level as expiry nears, indicating that option writers might be positioned for an upward move.

## FII positioning — what the big money did today

Foreign Institutional Investors (FIIs) are major participants in the Indian derivatives market, and their positioning often provides insights into broader market sentiment. Today, the FII Index futures long ratio stands at 9.3% (29,443 long contracts versus 286,343 short contracts). This is a significantly low long ratio, indicating a predominantly bearish stance from FIIs in index futures. Historically, such a low long ratio has tended to coincide with a cautious or bearish outlook from FIIs on the broader market.

In stock futures, the FII long ratio is 53.8% (3,804,985 long contracts versus 3,270,416 short contracts). This indicates a relatively balanced to slightly bullish positioning in individual stock futures, suggesting selective optimism in specific stocks despite the overall caution in index futures.

## What this combination historically tends to mean for tomorrow

Today's options data presents a nuanced picture. The Nifty 50's strong bullish bias from fresh put writing and its position above key support levels suggests underlying strength. Sensex and Bank Nifty also show mildly bullish leanings from their options structures, despite some call writing at higher levels. Historically, when domestic options data indicates a bullish inclination, particularly through heavy put writing, but FIIs maintain a significantly low long ratio in index futures, the market has tended to exhibit a complex dynamic. This scenario has often coincided with sessions where the market attempts to consolidate or move higher, but with institutional selling pressure potentially capping significant upward momentum. The divergence between the options writers' expectations and FIIs' futures positioning often suggests a battle between different market participants' views, potentially leading to volatility or range-bound movement as these forces contend.

## How to read this yourself daily

Understanding these market signals daily can empower you to interpret market movements better. You can start by looking at the Open Interest data for Nifty, Sensex, and Bank Nifty options. The National Stock Exchange (NSE) website provides free access to option chain data under the "Derivatives" section. Look for the "Option Chain" link for each index.

Once you have the option chain, identify the strike prices with the highest Call OI and highest Put OI. These will give you an idea of the immediate resistance and support levels. Next, observe the "Change in OI" column for both calls and puts. Significant increases in Put OI at lower strikes, especially near the current spot price, suggest strong support being built. Conversely, large increases in Call OI at higher strikes indicate resistance. You can also calculate the Put-Call Ratio (PCR) by dividing the total Put OI by the total Call OI, and do the same for the "Change in OI" to understand the fresh flow. Many stockbrokers also provide these data points and even calculate PCR and Max Pain for their clients, making it easier to track these indicators. Combining these observations with FII derivative data, also available on the NSE website, can provide a more comprehensive view of market positioning.
